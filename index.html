Predicting Sepsis: The Race Against Time

Sepsis is a life-threatening medical emergency triggered by an extreme, dysregulated immune response to infection. In the high-stakes environment of the Intensive Care Unit (ICU), sepsis is a primary driver of mortality because of its deceptive and rapid progression; for every hour that targeted treatment is delayed, the risk of death increases by approximately 7.6%.

Developing a predictive model is essential because it transforms the ICU from a reactive environment to a proactive one. By leveraging real-time physiological data to identify high-risk patterns 1–4 hours before clinical onset, our model provides a critical window for physicians to initiate life-saving interventions, improving patient survival rates.

Project Abstract

MIMIC-IV Based Research

"Sepsis is a leading cause of mortality in intensive care units (ICUs), where timely recognition is critical for improving patient outcomes. In this study, we develop an ICU-hour–level early warning model using the MIMIC-IV database... Leveraging structured EHR data, we construct a temporally aligned prediction framework that estimates sepsis onset within a forward 1–4 hour window using only routinely collected vital signs."

Multimorbidity Phenotypes

Addressing the "One-Size-Fits-All" limitation in clinical prediction.

Beyond Raw Vital Signs

Most septic patients possess multiple chronic diseases which cause their bodies to respond differently when an infection begins. While standard systems treat all patients as if they deteriorate identically, our framework uses Latent Class Analysis (LCA) to identify shared chronic disease patterns.

This approach allows us to investigate whether "silent deteriorators", patients who show only subtle warning signs, can be better identified by understanding their baseline multimorbidity phenotypes.

Subgroup 1
Cardiopulmonary

High-risk respiratory and lung-related deterioration signatures.

Subgroup 2
Cardiac

Specific heart-related physiological trajectories.

Subgroup 3
Hepatic/Addiction

Metabolic instability common in liver-related chronic conditions.

Subgroup 4
Complicated Diabetes

Unique glucose and renal trajectories following infection.

Subgroup 5
Uncomplicated Diabetes

A baseline diabetic phenotype with more stable deterioration profiles.

Subgroup 6
Young

Patients with fewer comorbidities who may exhibit rapid, acute onset.

LCA Modeling Framework

By combining phenotype modeling with pre-onset trajectory analysis, we test whether predictive performance varies meaningfully across chronic disease groups.

Layered Modeling: Vital signs capture short-term acute changes, while LCA subgroups encode longer-term baseline vulnerability.
